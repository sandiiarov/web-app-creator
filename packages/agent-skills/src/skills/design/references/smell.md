# Smell

Use smell to detect and treat choices that make a landing page look recycled, generated, or detached from its brief. Diagnosis names visible evidence. Treatment applies only when the user asks for changes.

## Landing-page tells

- **Tech gradient:** blue-violet, indigo-cyan, or purple-teal gloss used as automatic technology identity
- **Category hue:** a palette predictable from the industry alone, such as navy legal, teal health, green payments, or dark-terminal developer
- **Equal feature tiles:** repeated icon, heading, and sentence cards with no priority or narrative role
- **Accent rail:** a colored side stripe standing in for real structure
- **Unearned glass:** translucent blur covering an unresolved surface or depth model
- **Statistic monument:** oversized numbers without source, comparison, or consequence
- **Icon topper:** decorative rounded-square icons that neither explain nor operate anything
- **Motion reflex:** repeated fade-ups, bounce, parallax, or hover theater with no communication role
- **Default type:** a familiar family with no project-specific reason, tuned hierarchy, or verified loading
- **Center stack:** centered headline, pills, CTA, mockup, and equal cards used instead of a content-driven composition

## Prompt drift

Wrong names, inherited logo marks, unrelated proof objects, recycled headline shapes, unsupported claims, and assets from another brief are more serious than stylistic sameness. Compare the page with the current name, category, audience, artifact, evidence, action, and exclusions.

The domain-default test asks whether the visual direction could have been guessed before reading the prompt. When it could, identify the reflex and the missing product-specific decision. Unexpected is not automatically useful; specificity is the goal.

## Treatment order

1. Trust, readability, accessibility, or responsive failures
2. Prompt drift in name, category, proof, claim, or action
3. Formulaic structure and equal visual weight
4. Default palette, typography, imagery, or surface treatment
5. Decorative proof and unsupported statistics
6. Unnecessary blur, rails, shadows, pills, borders, or motion
7. Small isolated tells

For each treated finding, identify the reflex, connect the replacement to a prompt invariant or visitor job, apply the smallest coherent change, and verify that the replacement belongs to the rest of the page.

Useful moves include:

- Build the first viewport around a domain proof object instead of a center stack
- Prioritize, reformat, or remove equal feature tiles
- Derive color from brand evidence, environment, material, or imagery
- Give typography a physical or cultural reason
- Replace decorative mockups and number monuments with sourced evidence
- Restore hierarchy through spacing, type, framing, or proof instead of effects
- Remove repeated motion that explains nothing
- Rewrite vague claims and CTA labels around the actual offer and action

A fashionable default is not improved by swapping it for another fashionable default. Structural treatment may expose spacing or token seams; resolve those local consequences without turning a focused cleanup into an unrelated redesign.

## Verification

Use screenshots for visible before-and-after judgment and source inspection for semantics, responsive rules, focus, and reduced motion. Remaining findings outside scope or available evidence stay explicit.

## Done when

- Every diagnosed tell maps to visible evidence
- Prompt drift and trust failures lead cosmetic sameness
- Every treatment replaces a reflex with a brief-specific decision
- The page preserves its original offer, action, and valid behavior
- The cleanup creates no new dominant tell
