# Writing

Landing-page words orient visitors, make a promise, prove it, reduce doubt, and name the next action. Use writing as a full mode when copy is the request; when loaded as support, edit only language touched by the active mode.

## Make the page specific

The first viewport should state what the named thing is, who it helps, and why it matters now. Prefer concrete nouns and verbs over category filler.

Replace claims such as “transform your workflow,” “unlock potential,” or “built for the future” with a specific capability, artifact, outcome, or constraint. Do not invent metrics, customers, awards, guarantees, or testimonials.

## Build the argument

A strong landing page usually needs:

- A clear category and differentiated promise
- Evidence close to the claim it supports
- Explanations that reveal how the result happens
- Objection handling proportional to risk
- Trust material with source and context
- A primary action whose consequence is clear

Do not add introductory paragraphs that restate headings or transition copy that merely fills vertical space.

## Name actions

Buttons and links should say what happens: start a trial, book a visit, view the menu, compare plans, download the guide, explore the collection. Avoid Submit, Click here, vague Learn more, and Yes/No labels when the action can be named.

Keep the same noun for the same concept throughout the page. Variety is not worth terminology drift.

## Write for real landing-page forms

For lead, signup, booking, or contact forms that exist:

- Labels remain visible; placeholders show format or example
- Errors explain what happened and how to recover without blame
- Pending text names the actual submission
- Success confirms the action and a useful next step

A static page needs no invented state copy.

## Voice and tone

Keep one recognizable voice across headlines, body copy, CTA labels, captions, navigation, form feedback, and alt text. Tone can become clearer around risk and warmer after conversion.

Use sentence case by default. Avoid exclamation marks as artificial energy. Prefer a shorter sentence, comma, colon, semicolon, or parentheses to an em dash in generated page copy.

## Accessibility and translation

Write link text that stands alone. Give icon-only controls accessible names. Describe the information an image contributes rather than saying “image of.”

Leave room for localization expansion and different word order. Keep full thoughts together rather than constructing sentences from fragments.

## Verification

Read the rendered page in order and inspect source for hidden labels, alt text, aria names, and state copy. Check long strings and narrow layouts. Do not claim conversion research or user testing that did not occur.

## Done when

- Category, promise, proof, and action are concrete
- No unsupported claim or invented evidence remains
- Every CTA names its result or destination
- Existing forms and states use specific recovery-oriented language
- Terminology is consistent
- Copy earns its space and fits the chosen brand voice
