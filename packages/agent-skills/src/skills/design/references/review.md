# Landing page review and finish

Use this guide to evaluate or finish an existing landing page. Match the user's intent:

- **Review:** diagnose and recommend; leave the project unchanged unless edits are also requested
- **Finish:** apply a final pre-ship correction pass to an already coherent page
- **Generated-pattern cleanup:** identify recycled or prompt-detached choices and replace them only when treatment is requested

A review does not silently become an edit. A finish pass does not invent a new direction or disguise structural failure with polish; use the iteration reference when composition or identity needs fundamental change.

## Establish the evidence boundary

Inspect relevant rendered screenshots and the current HTML. Separate visible evidence from source evidence and mark unavailable checks as unverified.

- Screenshots can establish visible hierarchy, spacing, wrapping, clipping, color, imagery, and the rendered state shown
- Source can establish landmarks, destinations, alt text, font sources, media queries, focus rules, reduced-motion rules, and hidden-state implementation
- Neither establishes exercised keyboard, touch, submission, disclosure, animation timing, assistive-technology, color-vision, performance, or prolonged usability behavior without a tool that actually runs that check

Do not describe a class, rule, or keyframe as visibly fixed merely because it exists in source.

## Review the visitor path

A quick review checks five landing-page essentials:

- **Intent:** the exact name, category, audience, and offer are clear in the first viewport
- **Readability:** hierarchy, measure, contrast, and copy make the promise easy to understand
- **Conversion:** proof supports the claim and the primary action names a credible next step
- **Responsiveness:** available mobile and desktop views preserve story, proof, and action without clipping
- **Trust:** supplied facts, assets, destinations, forms, and accessibility cues appear credible and complete

For a thorough review, read the page in order:

1. What is this and who is it for?
2. What specific promise is made?
3. What proof can be inspected?
4. What doubt or risk is answered?
5. What should happen next?

Then inspect:

- First-viewport category, point of view, promise, proof, and action
- Sequence of claim, explanation, evidence, objection handling, trust, and conversion
- Proof quality and proximity to the claim it supports
- Coherence among type, color, imagery, layout, surfaces, words, and motion
- Navigation, CTA destinations, real forms, disclosures, demos, focus, and responsive behavior where present
- Exact names, supplied assets, required content, exclusions, and absence of material inherited from another brief

A polished page that ignores the prompt remains unsuccessful.

## Detect generated and prompt-detached patterns

Look for visible evidence rather than treating a style as inherently wrong:

- Blue-violet technology gradients or palettes predicted by industry alone
- A centered headline, pills, CTA, generic mockup, and equal feature-card grid
- Repeated icon-heading-sentence tiles with no hierarchy or narrative role
- Decorative icon toppers, accent rails, glass, glow, or nested cards without structural purpose
- Oversized statistics without source, comparison, or consequence
- Familiar type with no project-specific reason or verified loading
- Interchangeable proof panels, stock imagery, vague claims, or CTA labels
- Repeated fade-up, bounce, parallax, or hover theater with no communication role
- Wrong names, unrelated assets, inherited proof objects, recycled copy shapes, or unsupported claims

Prompt drift in name, category, proof, claim, action, or supplied assets is more serious than stylistic sameness. Specificity is the goal; replacing one fashionable default with another is not treatment.

## Prioritize findings

Order findings by visitor and business impact:

1. Trust, readability, accessibility, or responsive failures
2. Prompt drift in name, category, proof, claim, or action
3. Formulaic structure and equal visual weight
4. Weak or default palette, typography, imagery, or surface treatment
5. Decorative proof and unsupported statistics
6. Unnecessary blur, rails, shadows, pills, borders, or motion
7. Small isolated inconsistencies

For each major review finding, state the observed evidence, why it matters, and the visible outcome that would resolve it. Prefer a few decisive recommendations over an equal-weight inventory. Identify the strongest quality and primary obstacle.

## Finish the real surface

When edits are requested, inspect only parts that actually exist:

- First viewport: category, claim, proof, and primary action
- Section transitions, rhythm, alignment, and content measure
- Navigation, anchors, CTA destinations, and footer links
- Images, aspect ratios, alt text, loading behavior, and broken sources
- Lead forms, mobile menus, disclosures, pricing controls, or demos
- Focus styling, touch targets, hover alternatives, and reduced motion
- Mobile, desktop, tablet when materially different, and wide-screen composition
- Page title, description metadata, favicon when supplied, and social metadata when required
- Long copy, short labels, overflow, missing content, and realistic edge content
- Terminology, punctuation, unsupported claims, and unfinished placeholders

Subtract before decorating. Remove effects, containers, borders, shadows, colors, copy, and motion that do not carry meaning. Resolve competing focal points and near-duplicate tokens. Prefer one clear physical language over several half-used treatments.

Small defects matter because the larger direction should already be settled: a weak wrap, clipped label, missing focus style, dead anchor, inconsistent radius, abrupt section boundary, or broken image can undermine an otherwise complete page.

For a generated-pattern cleanup, connect each replacement to a prompt invariant or visitor job and apply the smallest coherent treatment. Structural cleanup may expose local spacing or token seams; resolve those consequences without expanding into an unrelated redesign.

## Verification loop

1. Capture relevant desktop and mobile views; include tablet when its composition differs.
2. Inspect source for behavior and states not visible in the screenshots.
3. In review mode, report prioritized evidence without editing.
4. In finish or cleanup mode, fix what the evidence exposes.
5. Inspect the changed surface again before claiming completion.

If screenshot capture is unavailable, say that visual verification was unavailable. If an interaction cannot be exercised, describe it as source-inspected rather than tested.

## Done when

- Every finding has visible or source evidence, or is explicitly unverified
- The strongest quality and primary obstacle are clear in review mode
- Prompt fidelity and trust failures precede cosmetic criticism
- Requested corrections preserve the product, proof, action, and valid behavior
- No obvious broken asset, destination, overflow, placeholder, or unfinished state remains after a finish pass
- Relevant mobile and desktop views are composed and unclipped
- Remaining limitations are explicit rather than hidden by completion language
